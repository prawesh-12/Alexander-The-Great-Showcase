Summary of Alexander the Great


Alexander the Great (356–323 BCE) was the king of Macedon and one of history’s most renowned military commanders. Born in Pella to King Philip II and Queen Olympias, he was tutored by the philosopher Aristotle, who instilled in him a love for knowledge and a passion for conquest.


After ascending the throne at the age of 20 following his father’s assassination, Alexander quickly consolidated power over Greece and launched a campaign against the mighty Persian Empire. His extraordinary military tactics led to decisive victories in battles such as Granicus, Issus, and Gaugamela, which ultimately resulted in the fall of the Achaemenid Persian Empire.


Alexander’s conquests continued as he advanced into Egypt, where he was welcomed as a liberator and crowned Pharaoh. Pushing further east, he crossed into India and won a significant battle against King Porus at the Hydaspes River. However, his troops, exhausted and longing to return home, refused to march further, bringing his expansion to a halt.


By the time of his death at just 32 years old, Alexander had created one of the largest empires of the ancient world, stretching from Greece all the way to northwestern India. His legacy is marked by the spread of Hellenistic culture, the founding of cities such as Alexandria, and the fusion of Greek and Eastern customs, which influenced art, architecture, language, and governance for centuries.
